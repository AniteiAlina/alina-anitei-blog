I spent a lot of Sunday afternoons doing things I didn't want to be doing.

Updating my blog's role title across three different pages. Fixing inconsistent link formatting. Rewriting my GitHub bio to sound like a person again after it had slowly drifted into resume-speak. Checking whether my About page still reflected what I actually do.

These tasks aren't hard. They're just slow. And because they're not urgent, they pile up. You tell yourself you'll batch them later, and "later" stretches into weeks.

Last week I used **Antigravity AI IDE** to get through all of it in a single session. Here's what I learned.



## What I Actually Updated

My personal blog runs on [Astro](https://astro.build/). It's fast, clean, and I like writing in it. But keeping the content consistent — especially across layout pages, the About section, and repeated references to my current role — has always been manual work.

I also wanted to refresh my GitHub profile. Not rebuild it from scratch, just make it feel current. The kind of update that signals "this person is still here, still building things."

With Antigravity, I described what I wanted in plain language and worked through the changes conversationally. The AI handled the file navigation, identified where inconsistencies lived, and made the edits. I reviewed, adjusted tone where I wanted to, and moved on.

What used to take a slow afternoon took about 45 minutes.



## What the AI Did Well

**Finding inconsistencies I'd stopped seeing.**

When you write the same bio twelve different times across a year, you stop noticing the variations. Antigravity flagged them without me having to grep through files manually. Small thing. Big time-saver.

**Handling multi-file changes without losing context.**

Updating a title that appears in multiple places — the homepage, the about page, the layout component — means making the same edit in several spots without missing one. That's exactly the kind of task where human attention drifts. The AI holds that context steadily.

**Giving me a first draft I could react to.**

This is subtle but important. I find it much easier to edit something that already exists than to produce the first version from nothing. Even if the AI's draft needed significant reworking, it broke through the blank-page problem every time.



## What I Still Had to Do

Everything that required judgment.

The AI could rewrite my GitHub bio, but I had to decide what to emphasise. Which project to mention. Whether to lead with what I do at Piwi.AI or what I care about more broadly. That's not a formatting decision — it's a positioning decision.

The AI could suggest how to restructure my About page, but I had to decide what story I was telling. What version of myself I wanted to put forward in 2026, given where my work has actually gone.

**That's the part that can't be automated.**

Not because AI isn't capable of producing something grammatically correct and structurally sound — it clearly is. But because the answer to "who am I, professionally, and why does it matter" isn't in any dataset. It's something you have to work out while writing, not before.



## The Shift Nobody Talks About Enough

There's a version of the AI conversation that focuses on replacement: what jobs will disappear, what skills will become obsolete, what humans won't be needed for anymore.

That conversation misses something.

The more accurate shift, at least in my experience, is this: **AI absorbs the cognitive overhead of tasks that are necessary but not creative.** The administration of creative work. The logistics of having a professional presence. The mechanical consistency that makes your content feel coherent.

When that overhead drops, you don't suddenly become lazy. You become more intentional. You have more space to think about what you're actually trying to say, rather than how to format the file it's in.

Machines handle repetition. Humans handle imagination.

That's not a utopian slogan. It's what good tooling has always felt like, and AI-native tools are just the newest iteration of it.



## What I'd Do Differently

I'd start with a clearer brief.

The sessions where I gave Antigravity a specific context — "here's the current role title, here's the new one, here's every place it appears" — were faster and required fewer review passes. The sessions where I went in vague and expected the AI to figure out what I wanted took longer, not because the tool failed, but because I hadn't done the thinking first.

AI doesn't replace preparation. It just changes what preparation looks like.



## Practical Notes If You Want to Try This

- **Have a list before you start.** Even a rough one. "Update bio, fix three pages, rewrite About intro" is enough to orient a session productively.
- **Review everything with fresh eyes.** The AI is optimising for correctness and coherence. You're optimising for voice. Those aren't always the same thing.
- **Don't automate the decisions.** Let the AI handle the execution. Keep the judgment to yourself.

The tool I used is [Antigravity AI IDE](https://antigravity.dev). It works directly with your local files, which matters if you're editing a blog or any project that lives on your machine.



## Where I Am Now

My blog reflects where I actually am in my work. My GitHub profile says something true about what I'm building. Neither of these things was urgent, but both of them matter — for the people who find my work and for my own clarity about what I'm doing.

The AI handled the mechanics. I handled the meaning.

That division of labour is worth understanding, because it's becoming available to more people, not just engineers. If you write, create, or maintain any kind of professional presence online, the friction is lower than it was a year ago.

The question isn't whether to use these tools. It's whether you're using them to create more space for the work only you can do.
